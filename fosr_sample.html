Thandi's business has revenue, repeat clients, and operational systems that work. What the FOSR profile shows is a business where financial visibility is functional but not yet forward-looking — she knows what came in last month, but the question of what the next 60 days look like structurally is not yet one she answers from a planned position. The one structural move that will shift the most is building a forward-looking cash position check into her monthly rhythm — not a complex tool, one regular practice that turns what she already knows into a decision input rather than a record.

First expansion move · Habit Design · Specific to Thandi and Mokoena Catering Solutions

Once a month — the same day, ideally the first Monday — open last month's figures and ask one question: what is this data telling me about next month? Not what happened — what does it signal about what comes next. This single practice, done consistently, is Signal Absorption. It changes what the data does in the business. Until it is in place, introducing any new financial tool will add complexity without structural traction.

Structural conditions noted — 2 flags
FX-03Pricing Floor Uncertainty

Thandi's catering prices are set against competitor reference and client expectations — not from a calculated cost floor for Mokoena Catering Solutions. This creates structural exposure: if ingredient costs rise or a client pushes on rate, there is no internal anchor to price from. Programme note: pricing content will land most effectively if it begins with cost-floor construction. The negotiation capacity is already present — the floor is the gap.

FX-09Reactive Posture

Response patterns indicate financial decisions are predominantly reactive — made in response to what is happening rather than from a planned position. This is a P4 structural condition, but it is being held in place by the P2 gap: without regular signal-reading, there is no planned position to make decisions from. Addressing P2 first will shift the reactive pattern downstream.
